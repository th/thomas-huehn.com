Legal & Licensing Workshop 2026 is coming up, and it made me remember something that a participant said in passing: many of the leading lawyers publishing about AI and the law have formerly been publishing about Open Source and the law.

And I noticed something else: even presentations that fell squarely into Open Source territory morphed into AI discussions in Q&A. And of course there were quite a few AI presentations anyway.

But why is that? I mean, on the one hand the answer is obvious. There is a definite pull factor at work here. AI is the current hype, companies are hiring for AI adjacent roles, it's new (unsettled legal questions!), it's cool. If you want to publish successfully, you had better pick a fashionable field.

On the other hand, I can also see a push factor, driving lawyers away from Open Source work. The legal questions have mostly been settled. There are a few fraying edges like the newer source-available licenses (that are not properly Open Source!) or the third party doctrine stuff that Software Freedom Conservancy has been pushing in America (and similarly the Rechtsmangel argument in the German MG case). But mostly Open Source law seems done. Nobody is seriously doubting the ability to enforce common Open Source licenses in court, even if most people prefer not to go that route.

But, by and large, Open Source seems boring from a legal standpoint. Most questions have been conclusively answered, both in academia and in the courts, some questions are unanswerable. When I asked an Open Source lawyer at [OpenChain & Friends](/what-i-heard-at-openchain-friends-2026) recently about new developments or cases she didn't really have news to share.

And also in license compliance tooling we're seeing this shift: license compliance is table stakes (even though one of the big players doesn't even handle dual-licensing correctly and wontfix). The commercial tools nowadays de-emphasize license compliance on the web sites and product pages. It's still there, but you'll have to dig into the product pages until you find those features. It's all about AI risks (including snippet detection) and cybersecurity (SBOMs) now.
